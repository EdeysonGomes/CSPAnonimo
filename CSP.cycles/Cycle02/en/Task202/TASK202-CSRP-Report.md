# CSRP-Report  
## Competency Specification Review  
### Task202 – Team Control


## 1. Introduction

This **Competency Specification Review Report (CSRP-Report)** presents the results of an **expert-based review** of the competencies reused in **Task202 – Team Control**, conducted as part of **Phase 2 (Expert Review)** of the **Competency Specification Process (CSP)**.

The purpose of the review was to evaluate the **adequacy, semantic scope, and activation modes** of the selected competencies when applied to a task that explicitly exceeds the expressive power of Finite State Machines and requires **memory-based computational models**, namely **Pushdown Automata (PDAs)**. In this context, the review examined whether the reused competencies adequately captured the cognitive, formal, and operational demands imposed by the task, while preserving semantic stability across reuse contexts.

In addition to validating competency alignment, the review aimed to identify **scope tensions**, **activation ambiguities**, and **refinement opportunities** emerging from competency reuse in a more theoretically demanding instructional setting. Task202 represents a relevant evaluation scenario for the CSP, as it requires learners not only to construct computational models but also to **justify model adequacy based on formal language theory**, thereby integrating constructive, analytical, interpretative, and justificatory dimensions of competence activation.

The expert review therefore focused on determining:

- whether the selected competencies remained semantically adequate under increased theoretical complexity;
- whether activation modes were consistent with the intended learner actions and assessment evidence;
- and whether observed tensions indicated the need for clarification, refinement, or controlled specialization within the competency set.

This review contributes to the iterative maturation of the CSP by documenting how competency reuse behaves when instructional tasks require explicit reasoning about the **limits of computational models** and the formal justification of design decisions.


## 2. Overview of Reviewed Competencies

Based on the CSP-Report for Task202, the following competencies were reviewed by domain experts:

- **C12** – Develop problem-solving solutions using Pushdown Automata  
- **C03** – Test Automata Using Simulators  
- **C13** – Interpret Rule-Based Notation  
- **C05** – Write a Technical Report  
- **C14** – Differentiate Classifications of Formal Grammars  

The expert review confirmed a **strong alignment** between Task202 objectives and the selected competencies (C12, C03, C13, C05, and C14), which together support a coherent progression from problem analysis to model construction, operational validation, theoretical justification, and formal documentation.

Task202 differs from earlier tasks by making the **evaluation of model adequacy** an explicit learning objective. Consequently, competencies related to theoretical reasoning and justification—particularly C14 and the interpretative dimension of C13—assumed a more central role, while remaining clearly differentiated from constructive (C12) and operational (C03) competencies.

The review also confirmed the CSP’s capacity to support competency reuse under increased theoretical complexity. At the same time, the task revealed that broadly defined competencies may present **semantic scope tensions** when activated within highly formalized domains, as observed in C13. This finding provides empirical support for future CSP refinements, particularly regarding clearer activation documentation and the adoption of controlled specialization when recurring semantic distinctions emerge across tasks.





## 3. Competency-Level Review Findings

### 3.1 C12 – Develop problem-solving solutions using Pushdown Automata

**Review Outcome:** Very strong alignment  
**Detected Tension:** None  

Experts consistently identified **C12** as the **core competency** of Task202. The task explicitly requires the design, implementation, and justification of a **memory-based automaton**, and the competency’s scope was found to fully support the modeling of stack-dependent constraints, such as volunteer-to-non-volunteer ratios.

The review confirmed that C12 adequately captures both the constructive aspects of model development and the analytical reasoning required to justify the use of a Pushdown Automaton in place of less expressive models. Overall, C12 was considered sufficiently expressive to support **realistic control and validation problems** while preserving formal rigor and semantic stability in reuse.




### 3.2 C03 – Test Automata Using Simulators

**Review Outcome:** Very strong alignment  
**Detected Tension:** None  

The review highlighted **C03** as one of the most clearly operationalized competencies in Task202. Simulation and validation using **[SIMULATOR]** are explicit task requirements and generate observable and verifiable artifacts directly aligned with the expected learning outcomes.

No scope mismatch or ambiguity was identified. Experts characterized C03 as a **stable and highly reusable competency**, consistently applicable across different automaton models and instructional contexts, particularly where behavioral validation and execution-based evidence are required.




### 3.3 C13 – Interpret Rule-Based Notation

**Review Outcome:** Partial alignment  
**Detected Tension:** Moderate  

The expert review identified a **semantic and scope tension** in the reuse of **C13**. While the task requires learners to interpret **formal rule representations**—particularly **context-free grammars** and symbolic constraints—the competency’s title and description were perceived as **overly generic** in relation to the specificity of the instructional context.

Experts noted ambiguity regarding whether C13 refers to:
- institutional or business rules,
- formal grammatical rules, or
- rule-based notations in a broader sense.

In Task202, C13 was consistently activated in relation to **formal grammatical and automaton-based rule systems**, rather than general rule interpretation. The review therefore indicated that the competency remained applicable, but that its broad scope made the intended interpretation context less explicit in theory-intensive scenarios.




### 3.4 C05 – Write a Technical Report

**Review Outcome:** Strong alignment  
**Detected Tension:** None  

Experts confirmed that **C05** aligned directly with the requirement to produce a structured technical report in **SBC format**, documenting modeling decisions, simulation results, and formal justifications associated with the proposed solution.

The competency was consistently activated as a **transversal documentation competency**, supporting the clear communication of formal reasoning and technical outcomes. No scope ambiguities or task-specific tensions were identified during the review.




### 3.5 C14 – Differentiate Classifications of Formal Grammars

**Review Outcome:** Strong alignment  
**Detected Tension:** Low  

The review identified **C14** as essential for supporting the **theoretical justification** required in Task202. Distinguishing between regular and context-free languages was central to explaining why FSMs are insufficient and why memory-based models such as PDAs are required.

Experts emphasized that C14 was appropriately activated at a **conceptual and justificatory level**, enabling learners to ground model adequacy decisions in formal language theory and the Chomsky hierarchy. The competency was considered well aligned with the task’s analytical demands, with no significant scope ambiguity observed.




## 4. Cross-Competency Observations

The expert review highlighted a clear differentiation in **competency activation modes**, reflecting distinct functional roles within the task:

- **Model-construction competencies** (C12),
- **Artifact-validation competencies** (C03),
- **Theoretical-justification competencies** (C14),
- **Documentation competencies** (C05),
- **Interpretative/symbolic competencies** (C13).

Experts observed that these roles remained clearly separated throughout Task202, contributing to a coherent distribution of cognitive and operational responsibilities across competencies. 

Unlike Task201—where identified tensions were primarily associated with **activation depth**—Task202 revealed a tension related to **semantic scope and naming clarity**, particularly in C13. This observation emerged from the consistent activation of the competency within a formal grammatical context, highlighting differences between general rule interpretation and interpretation grounded in formal language theory.
