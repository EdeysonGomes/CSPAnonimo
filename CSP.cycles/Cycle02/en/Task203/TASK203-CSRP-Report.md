# **Competency Specification Review Report (CSRP) – Task203: Parking Control**

## 1. Introduction

The **CSRP for Task203 – Parking Control** was conducted through a **technical–pedagogical expert review** focused on the **coherence, adequacy, and reuse of competencies** specified in the corresponding CSP-Report.

The review addressed exclusively the **conceptual and methodological structure of the task**, examining the alignment between task demands, selected competencies, and their activated cognitive roles. No personal, behavioral, or identifiable participant data were collected or analyzed during this process.

The review aimed to determine whether the selected competencies:

- adequately support the **computational expressiveness required by the task**;
- present clearly distinguishable **functional roles** (constructive, justificatory, analytical, or transversal);
- and remain consistent with the evolving methodological principles of the CSP, particularly those consolidated through refinement cycles in **TASK201** and **TASK202**.

In this context, Task203 constitutes an important validation scenario, as it extends the progression of computational models toward full computational expressiveness while operating over an already stabilized competency set.



## 2. Overall Assessment of the CSP-Report

Experts considered the **CSP-Report of Task203** to be **conceptually robust and methodologically mature**. The task was consistently recognized as a **natural progression** in the instructional sequence, extending prior tasks by requiring computational models capable of managing **multiple interdependent counters and control flows**.

The explicit justification for adopting **Turing Machines or suitable variants** was evaluated as theoretically sound, well aligned with the **Chomsky Hierarchy**, and consistent with the **model adequacy principle** established in earlier CSP applications.

Overall, the CSP-Report demonstrates:

- strong alignment between **problem semantics and computational model selection**;
- coherent articulation of **learning objectives, knowledge requirements, and expected artifacts**;
- and a clear functional separation between **constructive**, **justificatory**, and **communicative competencies**.

Experts noted that, unlike earlier tasks where competency scope tensions emerged from increasing theoretical demands, Task203 operates over a competency configuration that is already structurally stable, allowing the review to focus primarily on activation clarity rather than competency adequacy.



## 3. Review of Competency Reuse and Functional Roles

### 3.1 Core Competency (C07)

The competency **C07 – Develop Problem-Solving Solutions Using Turing Machines** was unanimously validated as the **core competency** of Task203.

Experts agreed that:

- the task intrinsically requires **unbounded memory manipulation**;
- multiple independent counters and control variables must be updated over time;
- and no weaker formalism (FSM or PDA) would adequately represent the problem constraints.

The activation of C07 was therefore characterized as **constructive and central**, with no identified semantic or functional tension.



### 3.2 Justificatory Competency (C08)

The reuse of **C08 – Identify Turing Machine Variants** was considered appropriate and well positioned within the competency set.

Experts observed that this competency fulfills a **justificatory and analytical role**, supporting:

- comparison between alternative machine representations;
- theoretical reasoning about clarity, modularity, and design organization;
- and informed model selection without requiring implementation.

No ambiguity or overlap was identified in the activation of C08.



### 3.3 Potential Tension in C09 – Apply Turing Machine Variants

The review identified a **potential semantic and functional tension** in the reuse of **C09 – Apply Turing Machine Variants**.

Experts observed that:

- the CSP-Report presents the use of variants (e.g., multi-tape Turing Machines) as beneficial but optional;
- it is not explicitly stated whether C09 is expected to be activated **constructively** (through implementation) or **analytically** (through justification or design discussion);
- and this ambiguity introduces a possible overlap with C07, which already covers the construction of Turing Machine solutions.

This observation does not indicate inadequacy in competency selection. Rather, it reflects an **ambiguity in activation mode**, consistent with patterns previously identified in earlier tasks, such as:
- the constructive versus analytical activation observed in **C04** (TASK201), and
- the scope ambiguity identified in **C13** (TASK202).

In Task203, the tension emerges from the boundary between **core modeling competencies** and **optional constructive extensions**, rather than from deficiencies in competency definition.



### 3.4 Transversal Competencies (C10 and C05)

The competencies **C10 – Test Turing Machines Using Simulators** and **C05 – Write a Technical Report** were evaluated as **stable and transversal competencies**.

Their reuse follows established CSP patterns:

- C10 supports **operational validation** through simulation using [SIMULATOR];
- C05 supports **technical communication and formal documentation** according to SBC standards.

No tensions or ambiguities were identified in their activation.



## 4. Cross-Competency Observations

The expert review highlighted a clear differentiation of functional roles across the competency set:

- **Constructive modeling competency** (C07),
- **Justificatory and analytical competency** (C08),
- **Optional constructive extension competency** (C09),
- **Operational validation competency** (C10),
- **Transversal communication competency** (C05).

Experts noted that, in contrast to Task202—where tensions emerged from semantic scope and naming clarity—Task203 revealed a tension related primarily to **activation boundary definition**, particularly between core and extension competencies.

This distinction reinforces the interpretation that competency tensions increasingly arise from contextual activation rather than from deficiencies in competency semantics.



## 5. Implications for CSP Refinement

The CSRP findings for Task203 contribute to the CSP in the following ways:

- They confirm the **scalability of the CSP** to instructional tasks requiring maximal computational expressiveness.
- They document a recurring pattern in which perceived tensions arise from **activation ambiguity** rather than from inadequate competency definitions.
- They reinforce the importance of **procedural clarification**—particularly explicit activation documentation—over structural changes to the competency set.

The review therefore consolidates the role of the CSRP-Report as an evidence-based mechanism for capturing how competency reuse behaves under increasingly demanding instructional contexts.



## 6. Summary of Review Outcomes

In summary, the expert review concluded that:

- The **CSP-Report of Task203 is methodologically sound and well aligned** with task demands.
- **C07, C08, C10, and C05** are appropriately reused with clearly defined functional roles.
- **C09 requires clarification of activation mode**, but does not require semantic restructuring or specialization.
- Task203 provides strong empirical evidence supporting the continued maturation of the CSP, extending the refinement trajectory established in TASK201 and TASK202.

The review therefore validates Task203 both as a **high-quality instructional specification** and as a **relevant validation case** for competency reuse under conditions of increased computational expressiveness.
