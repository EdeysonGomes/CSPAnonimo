# **CSP Review Adjustments Report: Task 02 – *Traffic Control***

## **Introduction**

The expert review process identified a set of **systematic and well-defined improvement opportunities** in the competency specifications associated with *Task 02 – Traffic Control*. These opportunities emerged from a structured, criteria-driven analysis conducted during the CSRP phase and were consolidated into **five core adjustment dimensions**, each addressing a distinct aspect of **conceptual rigor, pedagogical alignment, and representational quality**:

* **Knowledge Granularity** — calibrating the level of detail to ensure conceptual precision and assessability, while avoiding unnecessary abstraction or aggregation;
* **Knowledge Appropriateness** — ensuring that all declared knowledge elements are **directly exercised and observable** within the scope of the task, thereby supporting valid assessment;
* **Controlled Vocabulary** — standardizing terminology and conceptual labels to promote **internal consistency**, semantic interoperability, and long-term reuse across competency specifications;
* **Bloom’s Taxonomy Verbs** — refining action verbs to accurately reflect **observable learner actions** and the **intended cognitive level**, in accordance with Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy;
* **Textual Clarity** — improving wording to eliminate ambiguity, redundancy, and overly prescriptive formulations that could obscure performance expectations or constrain instructional flexibility.

In response to these findings, a set of **targeted and bounded revisions** was implemented during the **CSP-Adjustments phase**, with the explicit goal of strengthening the **semantic stability, instructional clarity, and reusability** of the competency specifications, without expanding their original instructional scope.

These adjustments ensure tighter alignment between the **problem context**, **learning objectives**, and **competency definitions**, while explicitly preserving the **foundational role of Task 02** within the CSP trajectory. In particular, the refinements stabilize the core semantics of competencies involving Turing Machines (e.g., C07–C09), preparing them for **controlled refinement or specialization in subsequent, more complex tasks**, while maintaining full traceability to their original specification.

Overall, this adjustment phase reinforces the role of the CSP as a **systematic, iterative, and evidence-informed process** for developing **high-quality, reusable competency artifacts**, capable of supporting progressive cognitive complexity across problem-based learning scenarios.




## **Review Findings and Implemented Adjustments**

### **Knowledge Granularity Refinement**

In line with the recommendations formulated during the expert review, the **knowledge classification framework was updated from CC2012 to CS2013**, which offers a **more fine-grained, hierarchical, and education-oriented taxonomy**. This transition enables a more precise representation of knowledge elements, supports clearer differentiation among closely related concepts, and strengthens the **semantic consistency, reusability, and interoperability** of competency specifications across instructional contexts.

The adoption of CS2013 also aligns the competency model more closely with contemporary Computing Education standards, facilitating integration with other curriculum frameworks and supporting longitudinal analysis within the CSP.

As part of this refinement process, terminology was systematically reviewed and aligned with **established professional and academic usage**. In particular:

* **“Requirements Analysis”** was **refined to “Requirements Engineering”**, reflecting a more precise and widely accepted designation that encompasses the full spectrum of activities related to **elicitation, specification, validation, and management of system requirements**.

These adjustments collectively enhance the **conceptual precision**, **pedagogical coherence**, and **assessment readiness** of the competency specifications. By ensuring that all declared knowledge elements are **explicitly exercised within the task scope** and **clearly observable in learner performance**, the refinements reinforce the role of knowledge components as **functional enablers of competency demonstration**, rather than as abstract or ancillary references.






## **Revised Competency Specification for Task 02**

### **Competency A Specification**


### **A.1 Competency Title**

    Develop problem-solving solutions using Turing Machines

### **A.2 Textual Description**

This competency refers to the ability to **design and implement computational solutions using Turing Machines** as a formal model of computation. It involves analyzing **well-defined problem requirements**, translating them into **abstract machine specifications**, and constructing Turing Machine models that correctly realize the intended behavior.

Learners are expected to define and coordinate **states, transition functions, tape symbols, and input/output behavior** in a manner that is consistent with the problem specification, ensuring **logical correctness, completeness, and internal coherence** of the resulting machine.

Within the scope of *Task 02*, this competency emphasizes the **foundational application of formal reasoning and computational modeling**, focusing on **direct modeling and simulation activities** rather than architectural decision-making or comparative formalism selection. It prepares students to employ Turing Machines as a **core representational tool** in Computability Theory, serving as a stable basis for subsequent refinement and specialization in more complex instructional contexts.




## **Competency B Specification (Revised)**

### **B.1 Competency Title**

    Identify variants of Turing Machines


### **B.2 Textual Description**

This competency refers to the ability to **recognize, describe, and differentiate variants of Turing Machines**—such as multi-tape, non-deterministic, or other extended models—based on their **structural characteristics and computational properties**.

Learners are expected to analyze **formal definitions** of Turing Machine variants and to explain how these models relate to the standard Turing Machine in terms of **computational capability and modeling convenience**. The emphasis is placed on **conceptual understanding and classification**, rather than on implementation or optimization.

Within the scope of *Task 02*, this competency supports the development of a **theoretical foundation** for understanding alternative machine formulations, without requiring learners to select, adapt, or deploy these variants in concrete solutions. It establishes the conceptual basis necessary for more advanced modeling and decision-making activities addressed in subsequent tasks.




## **Competency C Specification (Revised)**

### **C.1 Competency Title**

    Apply Turing Machine variants



### **C.2 Textual Description**

This competency refers to the ability to **apply predefined variants of Turing Machines**—such as multi-tape or non-deterministic models—to represent or simulate computational solutions for **well-defined problems**, based on given specifications.

Learners are expected to analyze the requirements of a computational task and determine whether an **existing variant** of the Turing Machine can be appropriately employed to improve **modeling clarity or structural organization**, while preserving equivalence with the standard model.

Within the scope of *Task 02*, this competency emphasizes **guided application and adaptation**, rather than architectural decision-making or formal justification of model choice. Students are not required to compare variants in terms of theoretical power or efficiency, but rather to **use provided variants coherently and correctly** in the context of a specific modeling task.




### Competency D Specification

### Competency Title

  Test Turing Machines Using Simulators


### **Competency Description — Revised **

Demonstrate the ability to **test, validate, and refine Turing Machine models** through **formal simulation tools** such as **[SIMULATOR]** or equivalent environments. This competency focuses on verifying whether a Turing Machine behaves as specified, ensuring **correctness, completeness, and conformity with formal problem requirements**.

Learners must be able to:

* **Execute Turing Machines using simulation tools**, systematically testing configurations, transitions, and halting conditions;
* **Analyze and interpret machine behavior**, comparing observed execution traces and outputs with expected results derived from the formal specification;
* **Identify and diagnose modeling errors or inconsistencies**, including incorrect transitions, missing states, or improper tape handling;
* **Iteratively refine the model** through structured testing and debugging, improving reliability and functional adequacy.

This competency emphasizes the **operational validation of formal computational models**, highlighting simulation as a critical mechanism for ensuring the correctness and robustness of Turing Machine–based solutions.
