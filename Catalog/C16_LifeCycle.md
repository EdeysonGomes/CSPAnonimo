# **Competency Lifecycle — C16: Interpret Turing Machine Concepts to Analyze Computational System Capabilities**


## **1. Original Definition — *CSP-Report (Cycle 1)***

### **Competency Title**

    Apply Turing Machine Concepts to Analyze Computational System Capabilities




### **Original Intent**

The original definition of C16 was introduced to emphasize the role of **Turing Machines as a general computational model** for analyzing the capabilities and limitations of computational systems.

At this stage, the competency aimed to:

* Enable students to *apply* Turing Machine concepts to system analysis;
* Support classification of problems as computable, semi-decidable, or undecidable;
* Provide a theoretical bridge between abstract computation models and practical systems.


### **Identified Characteristics (Cycle 1)**

* Strong theoretical grounding in **Computation Theory**;
* Use of the verb *Apply*, suggesting formal modeling or simulation;
* Implicit expectation of **Turing Machine construction or manipulation**;
* Significant conceptual overlap with the competency focused on the **Halting Problem** (C15);
* Limited clarity regarding **independent observable evidence**.



## **2. Expert Review — *CSRP-Report (Cycle 1)***

### **Key Reviewer Findings**

During the Expert Review phase, reviewers highlighted the following issues:

* **Insufficient task evidence**
  The activities and artifacts did not require explicit construction, simulation, or formal application of Turing Machines.

* **Semantic redundancy**
  Much of the reasoning attributed to C16 was already necessary to demonstrate understanding of the Halting Problem (C15).

* **Inflated cognitive level**
  The use of *Apply* was considered misleading, as the competency was demonstrated primarily through **interpretation and explanation**, not formal application.

* **Assessment ambiguity**
  Reviewers noted the absence of distinct evidence that would allow C16 to be assessed independently from C15.



### **Reviewer Recommendations**

Experts recommended that C16 should:

* **Not be treated as a core competency**;
* Be reframed as an **interpretative and analytical competency**;
* Avoid formal modeling or simulation requirements;
* Serve as **supporting or extension knowledge**, complementing core computability competencies.



### **Decision Threshold**

**CSRP Outcome (Cycle 1):**
**Approved with Downgrading and Scope Refinement**



## **3. Implemented Adjustments — *CSP-Adjustments***

### **Declared Changes**

Based on the CSRP recommendations, the following adjustments were applied:

* **Verb refinement**

  * *Apply* → **Interpret**, to reflect conceptual rather than constructive activity.

* **Scope restriction**

  * Removal of any implication of Turing Machine construction, simulation, or encoding.

* **Role reclassification**

  * C16 reclassified as **supporting / extension**, not core.

* **Redundancy control**

  * C16 explicitly positioned as complementary to C15, not as an independent anchor competency.


### Final Activation (Adjusted)

* **ActivationConstraint**: `optional` or `conditional`
* **ActivationMode**: `interpretative`
* **ActivationRole**: `supporting` or `extension`



## 4. Final Version — *Cycle 2*

### Competency Title

    Interpret Turing Machine Concepts to Analyze Computational System Capabilities


### Refined Textual Description 

This competency refers to the ability to **interpret and conceptually use core ideas from the Turing Machine model** to analyze the **capabilities and inherent limitations of computational systems**.

Learners are expected to use Turing Machines as a **theoretical reference model** to reason about **what classes of problems can or cannot be decided algorithmically**, and to explain how properties such as **Turing-completeness** determine the expressive power and limits of computational systems.

Rather than constructing or simulating Turing Machines, this competency emphasizes **conceptual analysis and interpretation**, supporting explanations about **computability boundaries**, **semi-decidability**, and the **impossibility of fully automating certain classes of program analysis**.

The competency focuses on **theoretical interpretation and analytical reasoning**, enabling learners to connect abstract models of computation with general properties observed in computational systems.



### Knowledge Specification (Final)
